
 Scientific claim: Weighed food records (WFR) result in poor completion since they're costly to run and impose high participant burden. 
 Participant Dynamics: Peer vs. Peer 
 Contextual Arena: The Didactic Arena (goal: to teach or learn) 
 Interaction Trigger: An External Mandate (a new law, policy, or trend) 
 Dialogue Objective: To Achieve Alignment (find a shared understanding) 
```
Speaker 1: So, the new nutritional policy mandates that we use Weighed Food Records for all dietary studies. What do you think about that?

Speaker 2: Yeah, I heard about it. Seems like a heavy lift, right? WFRs are notoriously tough to manage and they're expensive.

Speaker 1: Exactly. And not to mention, they impose a high burden on participants. People are expected to weigh all their food? That's a lot to ask.

Speaker 2: Right. Plus, the cost factor. Running a study with WFRs isn't cheap. The extra resources needed could be spent elsewhere, like on more participants or longer study durations.

Speaker 1: I agree. But the policy aims for precision. They claim WFRs provide the most accurate dietary data.

Speaker 2: True, but if participants can't complete them, is the accuracy worth it? High dropout rates could skew the results more than less precise methods.

Speaker 1: Good point. We need a balance between accuracy and feasibility. Maybe a mixed-method approach could work?

Speaker 2: Like combining WFRs with 24-hour recalls or food diaries? That might reduce participant burden while still collecting accurate data.

Speaker 1: Exactly. We should propose this to the team. It could align us with the policy while addressing practicality.

Speaker 2: Agreed. We should also think about leveraging technology, like mobile apps, to make the process smoother for participants.

Speaker 1: Yes, using an app could streamline data collection and potentially reduce costs. Plus, people are more likely to use something that's convenient.

Speaker 2: Let's draft a proposal. If we can show a viable alternative that aligns with the policy's goals, we might get some traction.

Speaker 1: Sounds like a plan. We need to get everyone on the same page before the next meeting.

Speaker 2: Agreed. Let's do this.
```
